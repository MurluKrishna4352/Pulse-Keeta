A proposal to unify retention, reactivation, engagement, and campaign intelligence across Keeta's international markets — turning scattered lifecycle data into predictive, human-approved action.

It is built entirely from publicly available information about Meituan and its international brand Keeta (news reporting, earnings disclosures, and industry research cited in the Appendix) and is intended to demonstrate product thinking, analytical structure, and cross-functional reasoning — not to represent Keeta's actual internal systems, data, roadmap, or strategy. Any figures presented as targets or models are clearly labeled as illustrative assumptions for the purpose of this exercise.

Six markets, one blind spot

01 · Overview

Executive Summary

The one-page version: what's broken, what we're proposing, what it costs, and what it's worth.

Keeta has spent three years proving a playbook — launch cheap, launch fast, win share with subsidies, then earn back margin. It has worked: Keeta is now Hong Kong's largest delivery platform by order volume, and a top-three player in Saudi Arabia within its first year. But the playbook's next phase — converting subsidized trial into durable, profitable retention across six-plus markets — is a fundamentally different problem, and it isn't one that low prices alone can solve.

Today, User Operations teams across markets manage retention with fragmented, market-specific spreadsheets and manual segment pulls, reacting to churn after it shows up in a monthly report instead of intervening while a user is still recoverable. Campaigns are largely blunt — broad discount blasts rather than the right offer to the right user — which is expensive at Keeta's current loss profile and slow to launch across time zones and languages.

PULSE proposes a single AI-assisted intelligence layer that unifies user behavior across markets, predicts churn and reactivation propensity before it happens, and lets User Ops, Growth, and Marketing teams act on those predictions through one governed, self-serve platform — with a human always able to review, adjust, or override what the model recommends.

02 · Strategy

Problem Statement

Keeta won the land grab. The next phase — converting subsidized trial into durable retention — is a different game, and today's tooling wasn't built to play it.

Keeta's expansion since 2023 has been genuinely fast: number one in Hong Kong by order volume, a top-three position in Saudi Arabia inside its first year, and coverage across Qatar, Kuwait, and both core UAE cities added in a matter of weeks in late 2025. That speed was won largely on price — free delivery, steep first-order discounts, aggressive vouchering. Management has now said the quiet part out loud: Hong Kong took 29 months to reach positive monthly unit economics, Saudi Arabia is targeted to get there by the end of 2026, and CEO Wang Xing has publicly pushed back on "blind" expansion and price-war-style "internal competition." The mandate is shifting from acquire share to earn it back profitably — and retention, not acquisition, is where that margin has to come from. Four structural gaps stand in the way.

1 · Fragmented, market-siloed data

Every new Keeta market stands up its own local ops team and ad hoc reporting. Qatar, Kuwait, and the UAE's two core cities were all added within roughly 40 days of each other in Q3–Q4 2025 — a pace that all but guarantees each region reinvents its own segment logic rather than inheriting a shared one. A retention tactic proven in Hong Kong has no systematic path to informing the Riyadh or Doha playbook.

2 · Retention is reactive, not predictive

Churn is typically noticed only after a user has gone quiet for 30–90 days. In a market analysts describe as "one of the most competitive quick-commerce markets in the world" — five well-funded apps (Talabat, Careem, Noon, HungerStation, Jahez) one tap away — a user who's gone silent for a month has usually already formed a new habit elsewhere. Reactive win-back campaigns are fighting a battle that's frequently already lost.

3 · Discount-led economics, at an expensive moment

Keeta's growth motion — up to 70% first-order discounts, waived delivery fees, blanket vouchering — is a blunt instrument. It's efficient for capturing trial, inefficient for retention: it over-subsidizes users who'd have stayed anyway and under-serves the specific users actually at risk. That's expensive precisely when Meituan's core local-commerce segment has posted back-to-back operating losses and is under explicit pressure to narrow new-initiative losses.

4 · Ops velocity hasn't kept pace with launch velocity

Meituan's own reporting describes a deliberate "four-country linkage strategy" in the Gulf. That's an aggressive cadence for any team still designing campaigns market-by-market in spreadsheets — configuration effort scales linearly with each new city, when it should scale sub-linearly with a shared platform underneath it.

Why this isn't just "spend more on retention"

More retention budget without better targeting simply scales the inefficiency described in Problem 3. The binding constraint isn't willingness to invest — Meituan's Q1 2026 R&D spend rose 22% year-on-year specifically toward AI — it's the absence of a system that tells Ops which user, which offer, and which moment will actually change the outcome.

02 · Strategy

Product Vision & Strategic Alignment

What PULSE is trying to become, the principles it won't compromise on, and how it ladders up to where the company has publicly said it's headed.

Vision statement

Every Keeta user's next best moment — a nudge, an offer, a fix, or simply being left alone — should be known before they ask for it, and every Ops team should be able to act on that knowledge in minutes, not weeks, in any market.

Five principles PULSE is designed around

01 — Human-approved, not human-replaced

The platform recommends segments, offers, and timing. A person on the Ops or Growth team approves spend and messaging before it ships. AI expands judgment; it doesn't bypass it.

02 — Predict early, act precisely

Optimize for days-before-churn lead time and targeting precision over blast reach. A smaller, better-aimed campaign beats a bigger, blunter one.

03 — One platform, every market

Shared infrastructure, locally-tuned models. A new market inherits the platform on day one instead of rebuilding reporting from scratch.

04 — Prove incrementality, always

No campaign ships without a holdout. Lift is measured against a control group, not assumed from engagement metrics.

05 — Trust and privacy by design

Regional data-handling rules differ by market (Hong Kong, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE each have their own regime). Localization of data handling is a first-class design constraint, not an afterthought.

What PULSE deliberately does not do

It does not set pricing or commission strategy, manage merchant or courier supply, or auto-send campaigns without a human approval step in Phase 1–2. Scope is retention, reactivation, and engagement intelligence for the end consumer.

Customer behavioral archetypes — what PULSE has to recognize

Illustrative segmentation logic, not real user data
HC
The Habitual Commuter
Low risk · protect, don't over-spend

Orders on a predictable weekly rhythm — often lunch on workdays. Price-tolerant within reason; churns mainly on service failures (a late order, a bad substitution) rather than price.

Recommended lever

Service-recovery triggers over discounts — a fast apology credit after a bad delivery protects this cohort more efficiently than an untargeted promo ever would.

DC
The Deal-Chaser
High churn-recurrence · cap the spend

Multi-homes across three or more delivery apps and orders wherever the discount is largest that week. Historically the easiest segment to "reactivate" and the fastest to churn right back.

Recommended lever

The model's job here is largely containment — recognize this pattern early and cap incremental discount spend rather than chase a cohort with structurally low loyalty.

NU
The New / Onboarding User
Highest-risk window · days 0–30

Signed up on a first-order voucher; hasn't yet formed a habit. Industry-wide, the steepest drop-off in any delivery app happens inside the first month.

Recommended lever

Habit-formation nudges (second and third-order prompts, curated restaurant picks) rather than repeated discounting, which teaches this cohort to wait for the next voucher instead of ordering.

QS
The Quietly Slipping User
Priority target · highest ROI to catch early

Was a regular; order frequency has declined over the past 3–4 weeks but hasn't gone fully dormant yet. Not visible in a standard "30-day-silent" churn report — which is exactly why it's the most valuable segment to catch.

Recommended lever

This is PULSE's core bet: a well-timed, modest nudge here is far cheaper than a large win-back offer after full churn. See User Journey for where this intervention actually fires.

Stage by stage

1 · Acquisition

Day 0
Today

User arrives via a first-order voucher or delivery-fee waiver — Keeta's proven playbook for winning trial in a new market.

PULSE's role

Out of scope by design — acquisition stays with Growth/Marketing acquisition tooling. PULSE's data contract begins capturing signal from first order onward.

2 · Onboarding

Day 0–7 · New/Onboarding User
Today

No systematic second-order nudge. Industry-wide, this window sees the steepest drop-off of any stage — and Keeta currently treats it the same as every other stage.

PULSE's role

Time-boxed habit-formation nudges (curated picks, a light second-order prompt) instead of a second discount — teaching ordering behavior, not voucher-waiting behavior.

3 · Activation

Day 7–30
Today

Whether a user "sticks" is discovered only in hindsight, at month-end, when it's too late to influence this specific user's activation.

PULSE's role

An early activation-propensity score by day 10–14, flagging users trending toward non-activation while there's still time to intervene.

4 · Thrive (habitual engagement)

Day 30+ · Habitual Commuter
Today

Healthy users are sometimes still targeted with blanket discounts they didn't need to stay — pure margin leakage.

PULSE's role

Suppress this cohort from generic promo blasts; reserve service-recovery credits for genuine delivery failures only.

5 · Waver Priority

Wk 3–6 declining frequency · Quietly Slipping User
Today

Invisible. Standard "30/60/90-day silent" churn reports don't catch a user who's merely ordering less often — this is the gap Problem Statement #2 describes.

PULSE's role

The single highest-leverage moment in the journey. The churn-propensity model is tuned specifically to catch declining-frequency patterns 10–20 days before full dormancy, triggering a modest, personalized nudge while the habit is still recoverable.

6 · Go Quiet (dormancy)

Day 45–90+ silent
Today

Caught by the monthly report; a large, expensive win-back voucher is the default response, often to a user who has already switched habits to a competitor.

PULSE's role

Reactivation-propensity scoring separates "worth a large incentive" from "structurally low-loyalty, cap the spend" — see Deal-Chaser archetype.

7 · Win-back attempt

Triggered, outcome tracked
Today

Campaign success is usually measured by redemption rate, not true incremental orders — so it's unclear how much of the "win-back" would have happened anyway.

PULSE's role

Every win-back campaign ships with a holdout group by default, so lift is measured, not assumed. See Experimentation Framework.

04 · Product

Feature Specifications

Nine modules, from the shared data foundation up to a forward-looking natural-language copilot. Priority reflects sequencing, not importance.

Unified User 360 Profile
Foundation layer
P0

One behavioral profile per person, stitched across every market they've used Keeta in, replacing per-market spreadsheets with a single canonical record.

  • Cross-market identity resolution (same user, multiple cities/countries)
  • Order history, frequency, recency, basket value, cancellation & complaint signals
  • App engagement events (opens, browse depth, search-no-order)
  • Consolidated support & feedback sentiment history
  • Single "churned" / "at-risk" definition shared across all markets
Predictive Intelligence Engine
Churn · reactivation · LTV · next-best-action
P0

The models that turn behavioral history into a forward-looking risk and opportunity score per user, refreshed daily.

  • Churn-propensity score with a 10–20 day lead window (see AI/ML Opportunities)
  • Reactivation-propensity score for already-dormant users
  • Predicted lifetime value, to size how much incentive a user is worth
  • Next-best-action recommendation (nudge, offer, service recovery, or leave alone)
  • Every score ships with a plain-language explanation, not just a number
Real-Time Ops Dashboard & Alerting
Command center
P0

The daily home screen for Priya and every Regional Ops Manager — what's happening right now, and what needs attention today.

  • Live retention, reactivation, and at-risk-pool counts by market
  • Anomaly alerts (e.g., a sudden churn spike in one city)
  • Drill-down from a market-level number to the underlying user list
  • Configurable thresholds per market, since a "normal" week in Riyadh isn't a normal week in Hong Kong
Governance, Privacy & Guardrails
Trust layer
P0

Not a bolt-on compliance checkbox — the mechanism that keeps "AI-assisted" honest, from day one.

  • Human approval required before any campaign spends budget or sends a message
  • Per-market data residency and access rules, configurable by region
  • Full audit trail: which model, which version, which human approved what
  • Frequency caps so no user gets over-messaged across overlapping campaigns
Smart Segmentation & Audience Builder
Self-serve targeting
P1

Lets Ops build a precise audience without waiting on a data analyst — see the live demo in Dashboard Wireframes.

  • Point-and-click filters on any 360-profile attribute or model score
  • AI-suggested segments ("users like your last successful campaign's responders")
  • Live, pre-send audience-size estimate
  • Segments are dynamic — membership updates automatically as behavior changes
Campaign Orchestration & Personalization
Act on the prediction
P1

Turns a segment and a recommendation into a live, multi-channel campaign — with a spend guardrail built in.

  • Push, in-app, SMS, and email orchestration from one brief
  • Offer-value optimization within a set budget ceiling, not open-ended discounting
  • Localized copy and timing per market (Ramadan-aware scheduling for KSA/UAE, for example)
  • One-click approval workflow before anything ships
Experimentation & Optimization Hub
Prove it, then scale it
P1

The mechanism behind Principle 04 — no campaign ships without a way to measure whether it actually worked.

  • Built-in holdout / control-group management, applied by default
  • Standardized significance and minimum-detectable-effect calculators
  • Phased, market-by-market rollout tooling
  • (P2) Multi-armed-bandit auto-optimization once a test reaches confidence
Insights & Reporting Layer
For Wei's Monday morning
P1

Turns the platform's data into the executive rollups and budget-defense material Leadership actually needs.

  • Automated weekly executive digest, one page, every market
  • Self-serve BI layer for ad hoc questions without an analyst ticket
  • Consistent methodology, so cross-market comparisons are trustworthy
PULSE Copilot — Natural-Language Ops Assistant
Forward-looking · Phase 3
P2

A conversational layer over everything above — ask a question in plain language, get a scoped segment, a drafted campaign brief, or a diagnosis of a metric change. Try the concept demo in AI/ML Opportunities →

Core Workflows

One system-level loop, and two operator-level workflows that run inside it.

The core loop — predict, act, learn

System workflow

Box 5 (Human Review & Approval) is the non-negotiable gate described in Principle 01 — nothing reaches a user without a person signing off.

Campaign creation workflow

Priya's view
01

Define the objective

"Reduce Waver-stage churn in Jeddah this month" — a goal, not a spreadsheet.

02

Review the AI-suggested brief

The Predictive Intelligence Engine proposes a segment, an offer range, a channel mix, and timing.

03

Adjust and route for approval

Ops edits as needed; Governance auto-checks budget, frequency caps, and market rules, then routes to the right approver by spend level.

04

Launch with a holdout

A control group is carved out automatically — no extra step required to measure lift later.

05

Monitor live

The Ops Dashboard tracks response in-flight and flags underperformance against the holdout.

06

Feed the loop

Results flow into Insights & Reporting and back into model retraining.

New market launch workflow

e.g. Bahrain, Oman
01

Connect the data

Plug the new market's event stream into the existing User 360 schema — reuse the pipeline, don't rebuild it.

02

Cold-start with a proxy prior

A brand-new market has no history — borrow model priors from the most behaviorally similar existing market rather than starting from zero.

03

Localize the guardrails

Apply the market's data-handling rules, language, currency, and cultural calendar (Ramadan-aware scheduling, for example).

04

Soft-launch in observe-only mode

The model scores users but nothing auto-triggers — validating signal quality before it touches spend.

05

Graduate to full operation

Once enough local data accumulates (illustratively, 4–6 weeks), swap the borrowed prior for a locally-trained model and enable full orchestration.

05 · Intelligence & Tech

AI/ML Opportunities

Six models that turn behavioral data into a decision, and a conversational layer on top of all of them.

Building on, not duplicating, existing Meituan Group AI infrastructure. Meituan already runs real-time ML dispatch at city scale (its "Meituan Brain" logistics system), large-scale recommendation/intent systems, an open-sourced 1.6-trillion-parameter model family (LongCat-2.0), and an agentic in-app assistant (Xiaotuan) elevated to the homepage in April 2026. None of this is currently pointed at user retention specifically. PULSE's premise is to extend that existing muscle into a new use case rather than building a parallel AI stack from zero.

06 · Measuring Success

Experimentation Framework

How PULSE tells the difference between a campaign that worked and a campaign that merely coincided with a good week.

Design principles

Holdout by default

Every campaign automatically carves out a control group. There's no such thing as a PULSE campaign without a comparison point — this is enforced by the platform, not left to individual judgment.

Incrementality over correlation

The question is never "did retention go up" — it's "did it go up more than it would have anyway." Before/after comparisons alone are explicitly disallowed as a basis for claiming success.

Pre-registered primary metric

The metric that decides success is locked in before launch, closing off the temptation to scan a dozen numbers after the fact until one looks favorable.

Guardrails run continuously

Message fatigue and CSAT are monitored throughout the test window, not just checked at the end — a campaign can be halted early for hurting trust even while winning on the primary metric.

The experiment lifecycle

01

Design

Define the hypothesis, the primary metric, and the minimum detectable effect (MDE) before writing a single line of campaign copy.

02

Power calculation

Determine the required sample size and holdout percentage, given the market's traffic volume and the MDE from step one.

03

Launch with holdout

Treatment and control are assigned simultaneously; control receives no PULSE-triggered contact for the campaign's duration.

04

Monitor guardrails

Fatigue and CSAT are tracked daily throughout the run, not only at readout.

05

Read out significance

The primary metric is compared at the pre-registered threshold — no stopping early just because an interim look happens to be favorable.

06

Decide

Scale, iterate, or kill — using the decision rule set in step one, not a fresh judgment call made after seeing the result.

07

Feed the loop

Winning and losing variants alike become training signal for the next weekly model refresh.

Significance thresholds — right-sized by market

A blanket 95%-confidence rule sounds rigorous, but on a market with limited daily volume it can mean waiting months for any readout at all. PULSE makes that tradeoff explicit and deliberate, rather than applying one rule everywhere and quietly starving small markets of learning.

Market tierExample marketsConfidence thresholdTypical MDETypical duration
Tier 1 — high volumeSaudi Arabia, Hong Kong95%2–3 pts absolute7–14 days
Tier 2 — medium volumeUAE95%4–5 pts absolute14–21 days
Tier 3 — early / smallQatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman90%†6–8 pts absolute21–30 days, or directional-only

† Tier 3 knowingly trades some statistical rigor for the ability to learn anything at all in a low-volume market — a conscious tradeoff, reviewed as markets mature past early-stage volume.

New-market rollouts follow observe-only mode before any experiment touches spend — see the New Market Launch Workflow for the full sequence.

Implementation Roadmap

Four phases, each earning the right to the next by hitting a concrete exit bar — not a fixed calendar promise.

P0

Discovery & Foundation

Month 0–3

No user-facing feature yet — this phase is about making every later phase possible.

Key workstreams: unify behavioral data into a single User 360 schema; stand up event streaming and the feature store; establish per-market governance rules (Saudi PDPL, Hong Kong PDPO, UAE PDPL); instrument the KPI baseline; align Ops, Data Science, and Legal on the North Star metric.

Exit criteria: a single User 360 record exists for every active user in at least two pilot markets; baseline D90 retention is measured and agreed as the starting point.

P1

MVP

Month 3–6P0 features

The first version Ops can actually open every morning.

Key workstreams: ship the Predictive Intelligence Engine's churn model (v1); launch the Retention Command Center dashboard; basic Smart Segmentation with manual filters; governance guardrails enforced end-to-end.

Exit criteria: Ops in pilot markets can see a daily-updated at-risk list and manually build a segment from it; the first holdout-based campaign launches successfully.

P2

Orchestration

Month 6–9P1 features

From "PULSE tells you who" to "PULSE helps you act."

Key workstreams: Campaign Orchestration & Personalization; a formal Experimentation & Optimization Hub replacing manual spreadsheet tracking; the Insights & Reporting Layer; rollout expands from 2 pilot markets to all live markets (adding UAE, Qatar, Kuwait).

Exit criteria: most retention campaigns across all live markets run through PULSE rather than ad hoc tools; AI-assisted lift is measured consistently via the Experimentation Framework.

Intelligence at Scale

Key workstreams: PULSE Copilot moves from concept demo to a real natural-language interface; advanced incentive/elasticity optimization; extend to new-market launches (Bahrain, Oman) using the cold-start workflow already defined; begin exploring the directions in Future Enhancements.

Exit criteria: the North Star metric shows sustained, holdout-attributable improvement; PULSE is the default UserOps tool across all markets, not a parallel option.

07 · Execution

Risks & Mitigations

Nine ways this could go wrong, and what's built in specifically to catch each one.

Data Privacy & Regulatory ComplianceHigh

PULSE aggregates data across markets with meaningfully different regimes — Saudi Arabia's PDPL, Hong Kong's PDPO, and the UAE's PDPL each govern cross-border movement and consent differently.

MitigationData residency rules are enforced at the architecture level, not bolted on later; legal review is a required gate in the New Market Launch Workflow before any market goes live.
Model Bias & FairnessMedium

A model trained mostly on mature-market data (Hong Kong, Saudi Arabia) risks mis-scoring users in newer, thinner-data markets like Qatar or Kuwait if applied naively.

MitigationPer-market calibration is required before a market exits observe-only mode; precision and recall are monitored per-market, not only in aggregate.
Discount Over-RelianceHigh

The path of least resistance for any retention system is "give everyone a bigger discount" — which would directly worsen the unit-economics problem PULSE exists to solve.

MitigationDiscount Dependency Ratio is a named KPI; the Incentive/Elasticity model's whole purpose is the minimum effective offer; Governance caps apply platform-wide.
Change Management & Ops AdoptionMedium

A tool Ops doesn't trust gets quietly worked around, no matter how good the underlying model is — a common failure mode for internal analytics platforms.

MitigationMVP keeps segmentation manual so trust builds incrementally; AI suggests, a person always edits and approves — control isn't handed over on day one.
Cross-Market Data LocalizationMedium

Some markets may eventually require in-country data storage and processing, complicating a single unified User 360 architecture.

MitigationThe Data Lake / Warehouse layer is designed for regional partitioning from the outset — a Phase 0 decision, not a later retrofit.
GenAI Copilot ErrorsHigh

A natural-language layer that misreads a query or states a wrong number plausibly is a real risk once Copilot moves past the concept-demo stage.

MitigationCopilot launches strictly as a read/draft assistant; anything that spends money or contacts a user still routes through Human Review & Approval.
Algorithmic FatigueMedium

The better PULSE gets at finding the right moment to reach a user, the more tempting it becomes to reach them constantly — risking opt-outs that undermine the goal.

MitigationMessage Fatigue Rate is a named guardrail KPI; frequency caps are enforced at the Governance layer, not left to per-campaign judgment.
Competitive ResponseLow

Rivals — Foodpanda in Hong Kong; HungerStation and Jahez in Saudi Arabia — could respond to more effective targeting with countermeasures of their own.

MitigationPULSE's advantage is the compounding measurement discipline of the Experimentation Framework, not one copyable tactic — a genuine but survivable risk.
Overexpansion Outrunning Ops CapacityMedium

Meituan's own CEO Wang Xing has publicly cautioned against "blind" overexpansion internationally, in the context of the company's early-stage Brazil entry — a caution directly relevant to how fast new markets should be added to PULSE's own scope.

MitigationThe cold-start-aware Market Launch Workflow is deliberately conservative: observe-only before any spend, graduation only after sufficient local data.

08 · Reference

Future Enhancements

Seven directions beyond the 12-month roadmap — directional, not committed.

Driver / Courier-Ops Intelligence

The same predictive-retention logic applied to courier attrition — a real operational cost in any gig-delivery model — flagging at-risk couriers early rather than only after they've already left.

Merchant-Ops Intelligence

Extend the architecture to restaurant partners: predict merchant churn, surface early signals like declining order-acceptance rate, and support account management the way PULSE supports user outreach today.

Hyper-Personalized Loyalty AI

Move from reactive win-back into proactive loyalty — individualized reward structures shaped by predicted long-term value and behavior pattern, rather than one-size-fits-all tiers.

Voice Support Integration

Extend the Sentiment & Feedback NLP model to call-transcript data, widening the "why are we losing people" signal beyond text-based tickets and reviews.

Expanded Copilot Autonomy

Once trust is earned through Phase 3's read/draft-only Copilot, a longer-term path could allow narrowly-scoped, pre-approved actions to execute automatically — always inside hard-coded guardrails, never open-ended autonomy.

Cross-Sell Into the Super-App

With appropriate consent and governance, predicted risk and value signals could inform cross-sell timing into Meituan's other services — turning single-vertical retention into whole-ecosystem retention.

Drone-Delivery-Linked Signals

Meituan's commercial drone delivery program — already live in Shenzhen and Shanghai — could eventually feed a new behavioral signal: whether drone-delivery experience measurably changes a user's retention pattern.
